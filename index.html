About Me

I like building things that people can feel, play, and remember.

My background started in game development, but it quickly expanded into XR research, technical prototyping, and systems-focused engineering. Right now, I'm most energized by building XR experiences where multiple people can share a space, interact naturally, and feel like the networking disappears behind the experience. I care about work that blends interaction, experimentation, and polish while still holding up technically under real constraints.

At Kennesaw State University, I've worked on VR research projects ranging from classroom attention studies to therapeutic applications. Outside the lab, I've led project teams, built multiplayer and horror concepts, and contributed to production systems work that sharpened how I think about software quality, observability, infrastructure, and the design of tools that people can actually use.

Research Output

Published work, conference posters, and research-facing presentations.

Published Paper

Saving the Polar Bears: A Collaborative VR Storytelling Experience Enhanced by Multi-scale and Multi-view WiM Designs

2025

Lei Zhang, Mason Prather, Devon Haynes, Feiyu Lu, Ying Chen, Joy Li | Proceedings of the 2025 IEEE Conference on Virtual Reality and 3D User Interfaces Abstracts and Workshops (VRW 2025), pp. 1546-1547

Co-authored a VRW 2025 paper presenting a collaborative VR storytelling experience about Arctic sea ice loss and climate engineering. The project combines an educational rescue narrative with gameplay and adaptive world-in-miniature interfaces, showing how VR can make large-scale environmental systems more understandable through navigation, interaction, and cause-and-effect visualization.

  • Published in IEEE VRW 2025 as a conference/workshop paper.
  • Focused on collaborative XR storytelling, environmental education, and 3D UI design.
  • Demonstrates research-oriented experience beyond game production alone.

C-Day Poster

UC-152 Posture Change in Immersive Experiences Using a Wearable Chair

2022

Mason T. Prather, Daniel Bodien | Kennesaw State University C-Day Computing Showcase | April 28, 2022

A poster project focused on detecting sitting and standing posture changes in immersive experiences through a wearable chair setup. The work framed posture change as a natural in-world trigger that could make actions like entering a vehicle or changing player state feel more physical and intuitive in VR.

  • Centered on posture-aware interaction in immersive systems.
  • Connected physical state changes to more natural experience transitions.
  • Represents early XR research that later informed my broader immersive work.
